A Disproportionate Need

Compared to the host populations, refugees and asylum seekers are:

  • 7x more likely to experience PTSD.
  • 3x more likely to experience depression.
  • 2-3x more likely to experience anxiety, bipolar disorder, and psychosis.

The Healthcare Paradox

About 25% of the >40 million displaced individuals seek safety in High-Income Countries (HICs). Despite the abundance of resources, severe undertreatment persists.

Resource Density

Globally, >90% of mental health care resources are located in high-income countries.

Staffing Ratios

HICs average 62 mental health workers per 100,000 population (compared to just 13 globally).

Funding Levels

HICs allocate 3.8% of total government health expenditure to mental health (vs 2.1% globally).

The Patient Access Pathway (Levesque et al.)

Access is a process, not a single event. Barriers occur at the transitions between these stages.

1. Objective Need

Clinical symptoms develop requiring intervention.

2. Perception

Recognizing symptoms as a mental health issue.

3. Care Seeking

The decision to look for formal medical help.

4. Utilisation

Reaching the service and completing intake procedures.

5. Adequate Care

Receiving high-quality, continuous treatment.

2. Methods

A rigorous scoping review of reviews based on PRISMA-ScR guidelines.

Search Strategy & PRISMA Flow

1. Identification

Searched 7 major databases covering Jan 2013 to Jan 2024. Initial search yielded 1,272 records.

2. Screening

After removing 654 duplicates, 618 records were screened, leading to 49 full-text assessments.

3. Final Yield

10 comprehensive reviews (8 systematic, 2 scoping) met all inclusion criteria for final synthesis.

Primary Inclusion Criteria:

  • Population: Adult refugees and asylum seekers.
  • Topic: Barriers/facilitators to accessing formal mental health care.
  • Setting: Resettlement in High-Income Countries (HICs).
  • Design: Peer-reviewed systematic or scoping reviews.

Characteristics of Included Evidence

Study Demographics

The 10 included reviews consolidated findings from dozens of individual studies (ranging from 11 to 69 studies per review).

  • Methodology: 5 exclusively analyzed qualitative studies; 5 utilized mixed methods. (Highlights a lack of purely quantitative synthesis).
  • Geography: High-income regions including Europe (e.g., Satinsky, Priebe), the UK (Pollard), Canada (Salam), USA, and Australia.
  • Populations: Analyzed adult refugees, explicitly focusing on subsets like women (DeSa, Firth), and mental healthcare providers (Peñuela-O'Brien).

3. Results: Taxonomy of Barriers

Analyzing patient-related constraints (Demand-Side) and systemic roadblocks (Supply-Side).

Demand-Side: Understanding & Literacy

Cultural Differences

Differences in mental health beliefs directly impact the 'Perception of Need' stage.

  • Somatic Presentation: Psychological distress is frequently expressed and interpreted through somatic (physical) terms.
  • Supernatural Attributions: Across reviews, attributing symptoms to curses, witchcraft, or evil spirits was consistently identified as a barrier to accepting Western biomedical models.
  • Misperceptions of Severity: General lack of knowledge leads to inability to recognize symptoms as requiring formal treatment.

Demand-Side: Stigma & System Awareness

The Fear of Stigma

Mental illness is often labeled as "crazy" or "abnormal". Patients fear severe social isolation, discrimination, and ostracization from their core family and community networks.

Lack of System Awareness

Poor understanding of healthcare systems and legal coverage leads many patients to bypass traditional pathways, resulting in an overreliance on Emergency departments.

Demand-Side: Attitudes & Trust

The Barrier of Mistrust

Past trauma and persecution deeply influence interactions with formal healthcare, preventing care seeking.

  • Mistrust of Authority: Fears that doctors will report them to government or immigration authorities.
  • Legal Repercussions: High anxiety that a psychiatric diagnosis will negatively impact pending residency applications or child custody rights.
  • Informal Alternatives: Due to these fears, patients prefer informal sources (e.g., family, religious figures) as their first line of treatment.

Supply-Side: Language & Structure

Systemic Roadblocks

System failures that disrupt 'Care Utilisation'.

  • Confidentiality Breaches: A severe lack of trained interpreters forces reliance on family/friends, compromising privacy and trust.
  • Gender Dynamics: Using male interpreters for female refugee patients significantly hinders communication and trauma disclosure.
  • Structural Isolation: Physical access is limited by isolated institutional housing and lack of transport.
  • Financial Chaos: Confusing insurance restrictions for newly arrived or pending asylum applicants block access to specialist care.

Supply-Side: Provider Competence & Bias

Provider Distress

Therapists frequently report uncertainty and their own psychological distress when confronted with complex, severe trauma cases, leading to avoidance.

Gatekeeping & Bias

Providers often distance themselves from "complex" patients. Patients reported facing insensitivity, stereotyping, and inappropriate racially charged questions.

Biomedical Rigidity

Providers often demand that patients discard their personal cultural beliefs and conform strictly to Western biomedical treatment norms.

4. Discussion

Analyzing the literature gaps and structural deficits.

Critical Research Gaps

An Asymmetric Focus

The academic literature predominantly focuses on the "peculiarities" and cultural deficits of the patients (demand-side) while widely neglecting how systemic structures and gatekeeping create inequality.

The Evidence Gap

Current evidence is almost entirely qualitative. There is a profound lack of quantitative data linking these specific barriers to measurable public health outcomes.

Risk of Stereotyping

Researchers risk over-generalizing entire patient populations by attributing low access solely to "divergent cultural beliefs."

5. Conclusion

Shifting the focus from persuading individual patients to redesigning equitable structures.

Actionable Strategies

Structural Prioritization

Implement early health insurance coverage that explicitly includes mental health treatment immediately upon arrival.

Task-Shifting

Utilize trained peer counselors from refugee communities to help patients navigate systems and offer support during wait times.

Interpretation Infrastructure

Guarantee adequate and sustainable funding for professional interpretation services to end the reliance on family members.
